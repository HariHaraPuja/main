Diwali Lakshmi Puja

Diwali Lakshmi Puja is one of the most important and widely celebrated rituals during the festival of Diwali, dedicated to Goddess Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth, prosperity, and good fortune. The puja is performed on the third day of Diwali, which is considered the most auspicious day for invoking Goddess Lakshmi’s blessings. The ritual involves cleaning and decorating the home, especially the entrance, to welcome the goddess. During the puja, devotees offer prayers, light diyas (oil lamps), and incense, and place sweets, fruits, and flowers as offerings. The Goddess is worshipped with various mantras and prayers, including the Lakshmi Ashtakshara Mantra and Lakshmi Stotra. It is believed that performing Lakshmi Puja on Diwali invites prosperity, success, good health, and the removal of negative energies from the home. The ritual is also done to honor Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles, and to seek blessings for a prosperous year ahead.

Dussehra Puja

Dussehra Puja is an important Hindu festival that marks the victory of good over evil, commemorating Lord Rama's triumph over the demon king Ravana, as well as the victory of Goddess Durga over the buffalo demon Mahishasura. The festival is celebrated on the tenth day of Navratri, also known as Vijayadashami. The puja involves worshipping Lord Rama, Lord Vishnu, Goddess Durga, or Lord Shiva, depending on regional customs. Devotees recite prayers, perform aarti, and offer flowers, fruits, and sweets to the deities. In many places, effigies of Ravana, his brother Kumbhakarna, and son Meghanada are set on fire to symbolize the destruction of evil. Dussehra Puja is also a time to honor weapons, tools, and vehicles, as they are believed to be blessed by the deities. The ritual is performed with devotion to ensure prosperity, success, and protection from harm, while marking the end of the auspicious Navratri festival.

Ganesh Sthapana and Visarjan Puja

Ganesh Sthapana and Visarjan Puja are integral parts of the Ganesh Chaturthi festival, celebrating the birth of Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles and the god of wisdom and prosperity. Ganesh Sthapana Puja: This is the installation or consecration of the idol of Lord Ganesha in homes or public spaces. The puja begins by cleaning and decorating the area, followed by the ritual of placing the idol of Ganesha, often made of clay, on an altar. Devotees offer flowers, incense, sweets (especially modaks), and fruits, while chanting mantras and prayers like the Ganapati Atharvasirsha and Ganesh Ashtakshara Mantra. The Sthapana symbolizes the welcoming of Lord Ganesha into the home and hearts, seeking his blessings for the removal of obstacles and the fulfillment of wishes. Ganesh Visarjan Puja: After the festivities and the celebrations, Visarjan marks the farewell of Lord Ganesha as the idol is immersed in water, symbolizing the god's return to his celestial abode. The Visarjan Puja is performed by chanting prayers and offering thanks for the blessings received during the festival. The immersion of the idol represents the cycle of birth and dissolution, signifying that Ganesha will return the next year to bless the devotees again. The event is accompanied by music, dance, and joyful celebrations, with devotees bidding farewell to the deity with love and reverence.

Hartalika Teej Puja

Hartalika Teej Puja is a significant festival celebrated by Hindu women, especially in North India, dedicated to Goddess Parvati and her union with Lord Shiva. It falls on the third day of the lunar fortnight during the month of Bhadrapada (August-September). The festival is particularly observed by married women and young girls seeking a good husband and marital bliss, and by women praying for the well-being and longevity of their husbands. The ritual begins with fasting, often without water (Nirjala fast), and involves a day of worshipping Goddess Parvati. Devotees set up a sacred platform, or "chowki," and decorate it with flowers, fruits, and incense. The puja includes chanting prayers, hymns, and the Hartalika Teej Vrat Katha, which narrates the story of Goddess Parvati’s devotion and penance to win Lord Shiva as her husband. Women pray for marital happiness, harmony, and the strength of their relationships. The day is filled with singing traditional songs, dancing, and performing rituals in honor of the goddess. The festival is marked by a joyous spirit, symbolizing devotion, love, and the bond between husband and wife.

Holika Puja

Holika Puja is an integral part of the celebration of Holi, the festival of colors, celebrated primarily in India. It is observed on the eve of Holi, known as Holika Dahan, symbolizing the victory of good over evil. Holika Puja commemorates the mythological story of Holika, the demoness, who tried to burn her nephew Prahlad, a devotee of Lord Vishnu, by sitting with him in a fire. However, due to her evil nature, Holika was burned to ashes, while Prahlad emerged unharmed, thanks to his devotion and Lord Vishnu's protection. The puja involves lighting a bonfire, known as Holika Dahan, where effigies of Holika are burnt to mark the triumph of virtue and faith over wickedness. Devotees gather around the fire, offer prayers, and perform rituals to ward off negative energies and impurities. The event is filled with chanting of mantras, singing, and dancing. Holika Puja is considered a purification ritual, symbolizing the removal of evils and the start of a new, vibrant season, as the next day is celebrated by throwing colors (Holi).

Janmashtami Puja

Janmashtami Puja is a significant Hindu festival that celebrates the birth of Lord Krishna, the eighth incarnation of Lord Vishnu. It falls on the eighth day (Ashtami) of the dark fortnight in the month of Bhadrapada (August-September), and is observed with great devotion and enthusiasm, particularly in places like Vrindavan and Mathura, where Lord Krishna spent his childhood. The puja begins with the fasting and recitation of prayers, hymns, and sacred texts like the Bhagavad Gita and Krishna Ashtakshara Mantra. Devotees typically observe a Nirjala fast (without water) or a partial fast. The worship involves decorating a cradle, symbolizing Lord Krishna's birth, and offering flowers, fruits, and sweets such as makhan (butter) and mishri (sugar crystals), which were his favorites. At midnight, the exact time of Lord Krishna’s birth, devotees perform aarti, sing devotional songs (bhajans), and chant mantras to celebrate the occasion. The festivities include reenactments of Krishna’s childhood exploits, especially his playful acts with his devotees and his miraculous deeds. Janmashtami Puja is a joyful occasion that invokes the blessings of Lord Krishna for peace, prosperity, and spiritual growth.
